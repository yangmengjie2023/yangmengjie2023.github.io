Research overview

My work asks how open quantum and wave systems behave when particles, photons, or collective modes can be amplified, damped, or transported directionally. The projects below are written for a broad physics audience: each starts from the physical question before moving to the non-Hermitian terminology.

Project 1 Published in Advanced Science, 2026

Can the size of a system change its topology?

Scaling-controlled non-Hermitian topology and exceptional-bound bands

Physical question. In ordinary condensed matter systems, topology is usually treated as a property of the bulk Hamiltonian. Changing the sample size may change finite-size corrections, but it normally does not define a new phase by itself. This project asks whether that intuition can fail in open systems with gain, loss, or directional amplification.

Main idea. Non-Hermitian systems can have spectral degeneracies where both eigenvalues and eigenvectors coalesce. Instead of treating these exceptional structures as isolated singular points, we studied situations where they form extended band-like objects tied to the boundary response of a finite system.

Result. We showed that the system size itself can control a topological transition. In simple terms, the boundary does not merely reveal a pre-existing bulk topology; the scaling of the finite system can actively reshape the spectral structure that defines the topological behavior.

Why it matters. This gives a way to think about topology in platforms where the effective Hamiltonian is not Hermitian, such as photonic, circuit, mechanical, and driven-dissipative quantum systems. It also makes finite-size behavior part of the physics rather than a nuisance to be extrapolated away.

Associated paper: Beyond the Non-Hermitian Skin Effect: Scaling-Controlled Topology from Exceptional-Bound Bands , Advanced Science, 2026. DOI: 10.1002/advs.202523989.

Project 2 Published in Communications Physics, 2026

Can boundary accumulation be reversed without changing the local couplings?

Transverse control of non-Hermitian skin accumulation

Physical question. In many non-Hermitian lattices, eigenmodes pile up near one boundary. This boundary piling-up is known as the non-Hermitian skin effect. The usual expectation is that the accumulation direction is fixed once the asymmetric hopping pattern is fixed.

Main idea. We asked whether a transverse degree of freedom can act as a switch. Instead of changing the microscopic hopping along the main direction, we changed how chains are coupled across the transverse direction and how the transverse boundary is closed or opened.

Result. The skin accumulation can be reversed by transverse size and boundary control. In lay terms, the system can be made to pile up on the opposite side even though the local directional bias along the chain has not simply been inverted.

Why it matters. This shows that non-Hermitian boundary responses are not only local properties of a hopping direction. Geometry, dimensionality, and boundary conditions can provide an additional control knob, which is useful for designing non-Hermitian photonic, circuit, or synthetic-lattice devices.

Associated paper: Reversing non-Hermitian skin accumulation with a non-local transverse switch , Communications Physics 9, 210, 2026. DOI: 10.1038/s42005-026-02608-5.

Project 3 Preprint on arXiv, 2025

Can edge transport be stabilized without ordinary symmetry protection?

Feedback-enforced edge states in stacked quantum spin Hall systems

Physical question. Topological edge states are usually robust because a symmetry or a topological invariant protects them. But if two quantum spin Hall layers are strongly coupled, the conventional expectation is that the edge modes can annihilate and the combined system becomes topologically trivial.

Main idea. We explored whether non-Hermitian dynamics can provide a different type of robustness. Instead of relying on symmetry protection, the system uses directed amplification and suppression: bulk excitations are not favored, while edge motion is dynamically reinforced.

Result. In an intermediate coupling regime, generic bulk excitations are redirected into helical edge transport. The robustness therefore comes from a feedback-like dynamical mechanism rather than from the usual symmetry-protected classification.

Why it matters. This connects topological physics to control and amplification. It suggests that in open systems, one may design protected-looking edge behavior even when the static Hermitian topological invariant alone would predict no protected edge state.

Associated paper: Beyond symmetry protection: Robust feedback-enforced edge states in non-Hermitian stacked quantum spin Hall systems , arXiv:2507.17295, 2025.

Project 4 Published in Communications Physics, 2025

How can interactions make bosons cluster strongly in an open system?

Interaction-induced caging and non-Hermitian bosonic clustering

Physical question. Bosons tend to occupy the same state, but strong and spatially structured clustering is not automatic. This project asks how interactions, lattice geometry, and non-Hermitian dynamics can combine to concentrate many-body states in a highly non-uniform way.

Main idea. The system contains an effective caging mechanism: particles are not simply free to spread across the lattice. Interactions and non-Hermitian pumping reshape the accessible many-body motion, causing particles to become trapped or concentrated in particular regions of configuration space and real space.

Result. We found strong bosonic clustering driven by the combined effect of interaction-induced caging and non-Hermitian dynamics. The clustering is a many-body phenomenon, not just a single-particle boundary accumulation copied many times.

Why it matters. This project connects non-Hermitian physics with many-body dynamics. It is relevant to quantum simulators, photonic platforms, and driven-dissipative settings where interactions and loss/gain cannot be treated as small perturbations.

Associated paper: Non-Hermitian strong bosonic clustering through interaction-induced caging , Communications Physics 8, 388, 2025. DOI: 10.1038/s42005-025-02274-z.

Project 5 Published in Physical Review Letters, 2024

Can a classical connectivity transition break a spectral symmetry?

Percolation-induced PT symmetry breaking

Physical question. Percolation is a basic problem in statistical physics: as more bonds or sites are added, small disconnected clusters suddenly form a system-spanning network. PT symmetry breaking, by contrast, is usually discussed as a spectral transition in systems with balanced gain and loss. This project asks whether these two ideas can be directly linked.

Main idea. We designed a non-Hermitian setting where the long-time dynamics depends on how connected the underlying random structure is. Below and above the percolation threshold, the effective gain/loss pathways are organized differently.

Result. The percolation transition can drive PT symmetry breaking. Equivalently, a change in network connectivity can move the spectrum from a real regime into a complex regime, making a geometric/statistical transition visible as a non-Hermitian spectral transition.

Why it matters. This gives a bridge between disordered statistical mechanics and open-system spectral physics. It suggests that connectivity, disorder, and topology can be used to control when gain/loss systems become dynamically unstable.

Associated paper: Percolation-Induced PT Symmetry Breaking , Physical Review Letters 133, 136602, 2024. DOI: 10.1103/PhysRevLett.133.136602.

Project 6 Published in Frontiers of Physics and Physical Review A

How do open-system effects localize collective modes?

Subradiance, chiral waveguides, and topological non-Hermitian skin effects

Physical question. In open optical and atomic systems, modes can radiate at very different rates. Some modes radiate strongly, while subradiant modes couple weakly to the environment and can live much longer. This project line asks how such open-system effects interact with directional transport and boundary localization.

Main idea. A chiral waveguide can mediate directional, long-range couplings between atoms. These couplings produce an effective non-Hermitian lattice, where collective atomic excitations are shaped by both radiation and asymmetric transport.

Result. In the atom-waveguide work, subradiant modes concentrate near an interface, while superradiant modes remain more extended. In the review work, we summarized how topology and non-Hermitian skin effects combine to produce boundary accumulation beyond ordinary Hermitian bulk-boundary correspondence.

Why it matters. This project line formed the bridge from quantum optics and open systems to my later work on non-Hermitian topology, boundary response, and many-body dynamics.

Associated papers:
Topological non-Hermitian skin effect , Frontiers of Physics 18, 53605, 2023.
Concentrated subradiant modes in a one-dimensional atomic array coupled with chiral waveguides , Physical Review A 106, 043717, 2022.
