Research overview

My work asks how openness, gain, loss, and directional transport can reorganize the usual physics of spectra, boundaries, and collective motion. The projects are ordered by publication date, with the newest work shown first. Each summary is written for a broad physics audience: first the physical question, then the method, then the significance.

Project 7 Communications Physics, 2026 Skin effect · Geometry control

Can a wave pile up on the opposite edge from where the local arrows seem to push it?

Reversing non-Hermitian skin accumulation with a non-local transverse switch

Physical question. In the usual cartoon of directional amplification, asymmetric hopping tells us which boundary should collect the modes. We asked whether this intuition can fail: can a different, transverse part of the sample decide the accumulation direction?

Method. We built a sequence of lattice models, ending with a designed non-Hermitian Kagome system, where changing the transverse size or boundary condition reorganizes the spectral loops and reverses the skin accumulation without changing the local couplings.

Significance. This turns the skin effect into a non-local switch: a distant boundary can control where amplification ends up. This is a useful design idea for non-Hermitian sensing, lasing, and wave-guiding platforms.

Related paper: Reversing non-Hermitian skin accumulation with a non-local transverse switch.
M. Yang and C. H. Lee, Communications Physics 9, 210, 2026. DOI: 10.1038/s42005-026-02608-5.
Project 6 Advanced Science, 2026 Topology · Exceptional points

Can topology itself depend on the size of the system?

Scaling-controlled topology from exceptional-bound bands

Physical question. In ordinary band theory, changing the number of sites usually changes finite-size corrections, not the phase itself. We asked whether a non-Hermitian system can have topology controlled by system size.

Method. We used exceptional-bound band engineering: near an exceptional point, eigenvectors coalesce and perturbations scale unusually with size. This creates topological phase boundaries that move with the system size, without relying on the standard skin-effect mechanism.

Significance. The work proposes sample size as a real design parameter for topological band structures. It suggests new ways to engineer photonic, circuit, or multi-orbital platforms where scaling is part of the physics rather than an unwanted correction.

Related paper: Beyond the Non-Hermitian Skin Effect: Scaling-Controlled Topology from Exceptional-Bound Bands.
M. Yang and C. H. Lee, Advanced Science, 2026. DOI: 10.1002/advs.202523989.
Project 5 Communications Physics, 2025 Many-body dynamics · Interactions

Can repulsive bosons be made to gather together at a boundary?

Strong bosonic clustering through interaction-induced caging

Physical question. Repulsion normally discourages bosons from occupying the same place. We asked whether directional non-Hermitian pumping and topology can turn repulsion into a mechanism for strong boundary clustering.

Method. We studied an interacting non-Hermitian SSH lattice. In two-boson configuration space, a local density interaction forms an L-shaped barrier; directional pumping then drives probability into this effective cage, strongly enhancing boundary clustering.

Significance. The result gives a way to pin correlated particles without an ordinary external trap. It also shows that topology, interactions, and non-Hermitian dynamics can cooperate rather than simply compete.

Related paper: Non-Hermitian strong bosonic clustering through interaction-induced caging.
M. Yang, L. Yuan, and C. H. Lee, Communications Physics 8, 388, 2025. DOI: 10.1038/s42005-025-02274-z.
Project 4 arXiv preprint, 2025 Edge transport · Feedback

Can edge transport be selected by dynamics rather than protected by symmetry?

Feedback-enforced edge states in stacked quantum spin Hall systems

Physical question. Stacked quantum spin Hall layers can become trivial when their edge modes hybridize. We asked whether an open system can still funnel generic excitations into helical edge transport without relying on conventional symmetry protection.

Method. We used intermediate interlayer coupling together with competing non-Hermitian directed amplification. The design keeps bulk modes nearly gainless while edge channels repeatedly feed one another, so bulk excitations are dynamically redirected to the boundary.

Significance. This suggests a feedback-based notion of robustness: the edge is not merely protected from scattering, but dynamically favored over the bulk. This may be useful for photonic and circuit platforms where gain and loss are tunable resources.

Related paper: Beyond symmetry protection: Robust feedback-enforced edge states in non-Hermitian stacked quantum spin Hall systems.
M. Yang and C. H. Lee, arXiv:2507.17295, 2025.
Project 3 Physical Review Letters, 2024 Percolation · PT symmetry

Can a percolation transition decide whether a system keeps oscillating or starts growing?

Percolation-induced PT symmetry breaking from topologically guided gain

Physical question. Percolation is usually about connectivity, while PT breaking is about real spectra becoming complex. We asked whether the geometry of connected topological islands can directly control long-time non-Hermitian dynamics.

Method. We introduced topologically guided gain. Chiral edge waves circulate around Chern islands; in weakly coupled layers with opposite gain directions, interlayer tunneling creates feedback once an island is wide enough.

Significance. When small islands merge into larger ones, the largest island can cross the width threshold for feedback-driven growth. Percolation therefore becomes a knob for switching a spectrum from real to complex.

Related paper: Percolation-Induced PT Symmetry Breaking.
M. Yang and C. H. Lee, Physical Review Letters 133, 136602, 2024. DOI: 10.1103/PhysRevLett.133.136602.
Project 2 Frontiers of Physics, 2023 Review · Skin effect

What replaces bulk-boundary correspondence when bulk states themselves move to the boundary?

Topological non-Hermitian skin effect

Physical question. The non-Hermitian skin effect challenges a basic habit of band theory: predicting boundaries from periodic bulk bands. We asked how this boundary accumulation reshapes topology.

Method. We reviewed the modified bulk-boundary correspondence, the hybridization between skin modes and topological modes, and the role of complex-energy topology such as spectral winding and graph topology.

Significance. This work helped organize the conceptual map for my later projects: when boundary accumulation, topology, dynamics, and interactions meet, the right question is often not only “what is the phase?”, but “what does the system do under open boundaries?”

Related paper: Topological non-Hermitian skin effect.
R. Lin, T. Tai, M. Yang, L. Li, and C. H. Lee, Frontiers of Physics 18, 53605, 2023. DOI: 10.1007/s11467-023-1309-z.
Project 1 Physical Review A, 2022 Quantum optics · Subradiance

Can long-lived light-matter excitations be funneled to an interface?

Concentrated subradiant modes in chiral atom-waveguide arrays

Physical question. Atomic arrays coupled to waveguides naturally decay, but some collective modes decay slowly. We asked whether chiral photon-mediated interactions can actively concentrate such long-lived modes at a designed interface.

Method. We studied a tilted one-dimensional atomic array between two chiral waveguides with opposite propagation directions. The geometry produces long-range, position-dependent, nonreciprocal couplings and an artificial middle interface.

Significance. The model separates slow, concentrated subradiant modes from fast-decaying, extended superradiant modes. It connects the skin effect to a quantum-optics platform and suggests routes toward robust photon storage and state manipulation.

Related paper: Concentrated subradiant modes in a one-dimensional atomic array coupled with chiral waveguides.
M. Yang et al., Physical Review A 106, 043717, 2022. DOI: 10.1103/PhysRevA.106.043717.
